Do year-over-year SEC filing changes predict returns?

A point-in-time, no-lookahead event study across the S&P 500, with costs included, out-of-sample, and against an honest null. Try the two grounded tools first, then see what the study found.

Try it: the grounded narrator

Search any of the 480 S&P 500 companies, pick one of its 10-K filings, and a server-side model (GLM 5.2, free via NVIDIA) writes a short memo from the study's pre-computed numbers only. It never computes a figure and never gives advice. It can narrate only real, measured filing events, so it can't be pushed into predicting or inventing anything.

Ask about the filing changes

Ask a plain-English question about a company's most recent 10-K risk-factor changes. The model answers only from the actual added, removed, and reworded sentences the study diffs out of that filing, with the source cited. If the answer isn't in those changes, it says so. Descriptive only: it won't predict, value, or advise.

What we found
The calibrated answer: no, the effect is weak and does not survive as a tradeable signal. On the full universe, point-in-time and net of costs, more added risk-factor language shows the documented "Lazy Prices" direction only marginally on the holdout (CAR[0,+21] p = 0.03), flips to the wrong sign in-sample, and produces a net-of-cost long-short spread of just +0.07% that is statistically insignificant (p = 0.51). That is a legitimate, credible result. The value is the harness that says so honestly, not a signal that doesn't hold up.

Why this is the credible outcome

A 30-name subsample showed a strong, significant holdout effect. The full 500 washes it out, a textbook demonstration of why out-of-sample, full-universe, net-of-cost discipline exists. The methodology is the point:

  • No lookahead. Each filing is aligned to the first session tradeable after its EDGAR acceptance instant; every feature uses only data dated at or before that instant, enforced in code. A pre-event placebo window confirms the signal has no leakage.
  • Two or more expected-return models. Market-adjusted, sector-adjusted, and a market-model beta estimated on a pre-event window.
  • Costs included. Every tradeable claim is reported net of a round-trip cost. A gross-only result is fiction.
  • Run the holdout once. Signals and specs were frozen in-sample; the holdout is a single look.

Not claimed: net_added is a blunt mechanical count. A diff-grounded LLM classifier sharpens it into "substantive new risks vs boilerplate." Whether that survives is an open, documented next step.
